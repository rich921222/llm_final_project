Deep Learning Models

===== Page 3 =====
Why Move from Traditional NLP to Deep Learning?
● Traditional NLP relies on hand-crafted features (e.g., Bag-of-Words, TF-IDF).
○ Formula is designed by human
● Often fails to capture context, word order, or semantics.
● Struggles with sarcasm, polysemy (e.g., "bank"), and long-distance
dependencies.
● Deep learning allows automatic feature learning from data.
● Enables more powerful, context-aware models.

===== Page 4 =====
Why Deep Learning Works Well in NLP
● Learns word meaning through dense vectors (embeddings).
● Captures syntax, semantics, and contextual relationships.
● Scales to large corpora and diverse domains.
● Enables end-to-end learning from raw input to prediction.
● Flexible and adaptable across NLP tasks.

===== Page 5 =====
What to Watch Out for When Using Deep Learning in NLP
● Data Hungry: Requires large, labeled datasets for training.
● Computationally Expensive: Needs powerful hardware (GPU/TPU).
● Interpretability: Deep models are often black boxes.
● Overfitting: Especially with small or biased data.
● Ethical Issues: May reproduce or amplify biases in data.

===== Page 11 =====
Embedding
● Consider a generic task, like trying to predict whether or not a user will
purchase something on your website
● One data point you have is how the user got to your site:
○ Search (e.g. google)
○ Advertisement
○ Friend
● Can you just assign numbers? 0 = Search, 1 = Ad, 2 = Friend? No…
● One-hot encoding: [1,0,0] = Search, [0,1,0] = Ad, [0,0,1] = Friend
● If w1 is large/positive, search users are more likely to purchase
● If w2 is large/negative, ad users are less likely to purchase

===== Page 12 =====
Same Strategy for NLP
● Treat each word as a category, and create a big feature vector with one
position for every possible word, e.g.
[1,0,0, …, 0] = “a”
[0,1,0, …, 0] = “aa”
…
[0,0,0, …, 1] = “zygote”
● We can then do our usual WTx + b computation where x = one-hot vector

===== Page 25 =====
Why CNN Is Not Enough
● CNNs are great for capturing local patterns in text, such as short phrases like
"not good" or "very happy". But they come with limitations:
○ CNNs only capture fixed-size n-gram features.
○ They struggle with long-distance dependencies in language.
■ Example: “The book I bought yesterday was amazing.” → “book” and “amazing” are far
apart.
○ CNNs are position-invariant, meaning they don't inherently understand word order.
● To model sequence and context over time, we need a model that remembers
previous inputs—this is where RNNs come in.

===== Page 50 =====
Limitations of Vanilla RNNs
● While RNNs can capture word order and dependencies, they have their own
issues:
○ Vanishing gradients: Difficult to learn long-range dependencies (early inputs get "forgotten")
○ Slow to train: Because each step depends on the previous one, RNNs are not parallelizable

===== Page 51 =====

● Ask your simple RNN: “On what day was Albert Einstein born?”
● By the time the RNN has “read” the whole article, it forgot what was at the
beginning!

===== Page 55 =====
GRU
● z(t), r(t), h(t) are all vectors of size M
● M is a hyperparameter (“number of hidden units / features”)
● This implies the shape of all the weights
● Any weight going from x(t) is D x M
● Any weight going from h(t) is M x M
● All bias terms are of size M

===== Page 56 =====
What is the GRU Doing?
● z(t) = update gate vector
● “Should I take the new value for h(t), or should I keep the old value, h(t-1)?”
● Vanishing gradient means the RNN forgets things in the past
● Now, we can explicitly remember the previous h(t-1) (If z(t) small)
● If z(t) → 1, then forget h(t-1)

===== Page 62 =====
Why Output Gate?
● 為何不直接將ct的值輸出作為ht?
● 長期記憶通常包含豐富且雜亂的資訊，直接輸出的話下一層會被迫接收大量當
下根本不需要的雜訊
● Ex: “Alice 是一個非常優秀的工程師，我昨天在會議上遇到她” （translation task）
○ 當模型讀到Alice時，ct 會記住兩個特徵：女性、單數
○ 當模型讀到“是一個非常優秀的工程師...”時，後面的預測層根本不需要知道Alice 是女性這件事
○ 這時，output gate會關閉對於性別特徵的輸出，不讓它干擾當下的語意判斷
○ 直到最後讀到”遇到...”，準備要預測代名詞時，output gate才會打開，把ct裡的”女性”特徵釋出到
ht，讓模型能夠預測出“her。
